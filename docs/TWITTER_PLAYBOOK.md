## 2 · Why X now, before the site launches

Fitness Twitter is an active, opinionated community. The people who follow HRV researchers, sports scientists, and honest programming educators are exactly FORM's primary segment — the self-coached intermediate lifter who reads Jeff Nippard's breakdowns and thinks most gym content is noise.

Three specific reasons to start now rather than at launch:

**Reason 1: Audience compounds, but it takes time.**
An account with 800 followers who followed because of genuine insight will convert to beta signups at 5 to 10 times the rate of a cold launch to 0. The audience you build over 90 days of pre-launch activity will be the early adopters who write the first App Store reviews.

**Reason 2: Build-in-public earns trust before you ask for anything.**
When the launch tweet goes out, an account with 90 days of consistent honest content has proof. When a stranger reads "FORM just launched," then scrolls back and sees 3 months of real development updates, real research, real doubts — they trust the product before downloading it. A cold launch from a 2-week-old account gets nothing.

**Reason 3: Beta recruiting is materially easier with a warm audience.**
`docs/RESEARCH_SCREENER.md` outlines the X DM recruiting flow. That flow only works if you have already engaged with the people you are messaging. An account that has been in conversations with serious lifters for 90 days can recruit 30 to 50 beta users directly from followers. A cold account cannot.

What X cannot do well (addressed in Section 12): drive installs at scale, replace long-form content, or substitute for App Store ASO. Keep the channel in its lane.

## 3 · Phase 1: Pre-launch (now through Beta launch, ~Q3 2026)

This phase has one primary goal: build an audience of 500 to 1,000 people who are the right demographic and who believe FORM is a serious product built by someone who knows what they are talking about.

Secondary goals: recruit 30 user research participants, generate waitlist signups, and surface product insights from the fitness community.

### Personal account vs. brand account

Run both, but understand what each is for.

**@form_coach (brand account):** Product updates, training science content, FORM-specific announcements. This account speaks in George's voice but stays close to the product and the science. Someone who follows this account wants FORM updates.

**@Rbit27 (George's personal account):** Founder perspective, building-in-public decisions, the Ukraine context, raw process. Someone who follows this account wants the builder story.

In practice, most content should live on @form_coach, with @Rbit27 occasionally amplifying or adding founder-voice commentary. Do not duplicate posts between accounts — they serve different readers.

Cross-post rule: if a tweet is a product update or training science take, post on @form_coach. If it is a raw reflection about the build process ("I almost scrapped the CV pipeline today because..."), post on @Rbit27 and let it stand on its own. Trying to run two identical feeds creates cognitive overhead with no benefit.

### What to post about in Phase 1

Five categories, in rough priority order:

1. **Training science takes.** Short, opinionated, data-grounded. "HRV doesn't tell you when to train — it tells you when to ask better questions." These build the expert signal without requiring a live product.

2. **Build decisions made visible.** "We cut the streak feature. Here's why." "We almost added a calorie floor of 1,200 — and why we didn't." These signal honesty and invite conversation.

3. **Research in progress.** Share what you're learning from user interviews before you publish the full post. "Third interview today. Everyone says the same thing about rest day guilt. Writing this up."

4. **Industry critique.** Honest takes on what's broken in fitness apps and why. This earns the credibility to later say "FORM is different." Must be substantiated — not vague complaints.

5. **Ukraine context.** Selective, not constant. When it's relevant to the build, share it. Not as a daily emotional hook, but as authentic context. The world knows what building from Kyiv means in 2026.

## 4 · Content pillars

Five recurring themes. Each should appear in the feed at least twice per month.

### Pillar 1: Building in public — the real version

Not "we hit X users" (you don't have users yet). Instead: decisions, tradeoffs, things cut, things failed.

Format: 1 to 3 tweets. Direct statement of what happened, brief explanation of why.

Examples:
```
We spent 3 weeks on a rest-day coaching mode and cut it.

The problem: there's no such thing as "rest day" for everyone —
some people do mobility, some do cardio, some do nothing.
Generic rest-day advice is worse than silence.

Victor doesn't speak when he has nothing useful to say.
```

```
Considered adding gamification. Streak counters, achievement badges.

We cut all of it.

Not because it doesn't work — it does, it drives engagement.
But it drives engagement through mild shame and loss aversion.
That's not the kind of trainer we're building.
```

Anti-pattern: "We just shipped X" without the why. Build-in-public without editorial context is just a changelog.

### Pillar 2: Training science — translated, not summarized

Original takes on evidence-based concepts. Not textbook summaries. The question is always: what does a smart self-coached lifter get wrong about this, and why?

Format: standalone tweet or 3 to 5 tweet thread.

Examples:
```
HRV is a signal of autonomic nervous system state, not readiness to train.

Those are different things.

A 15% HRV drop tells you something stressed your system.
It doesn't tell you whether that's travel, alcohol, illness, or heavy legs.

Context is the variable HRV doesn't supply.
```

```
Progressive overload is not "add weight every session."

That's linear progression, and it works — for beginners, for 6-16 weeks.

After that, overload happens across multiple dimensions:
volume, frequency, density, proximity to failure, range of motion.

The weight on the bar is one dial of seven.
```

Sources: cite them when you make a specific empirical claim. DOI in thread footer is fine. "Studies show" is not.

### Pillar 3: Honest product preview — Victor's voice

Preview what coaching from Victor sounds like. Short fragments. Real cues, not marketing language.

Format: standalone tweet, formatted as Victor speaking. No framing needed beyond a one-line setup.

Examples:
```
What Victor actually says after a missed session:

"Missed Wednesday. You still have Thursday and Saturday.
This week isn't broken. Pick it up."

Not: "Don't break the streak! Your future self is counting on you!"
```

```
Victor after a PR:

"122kg. You hit it clean. Note that your warm-up was longer — keep that."

Not: "BEAST MODE ACTIVATED. You're unstoppable."
```

These posts do two things: demonstrate the product's voice before anyone can use it, and make the contrast with generic fitness apps visceral.

### Pillar 4: FORM system and decisions

Technical and product transparency. How the camera pipeline works. Why the pricing model is what it is. What we learned from user research. What we almost built.

This pillar is primarily for the fitness tech and indie developer audience, who cross-pollinate with fitness Twitter more than people assume.

Examples:
```
Camera-based form analysis via phone: what's actually hard.

Not the detection — pose estimation is a solved problem.
The hard part: distinguishing "bad form" from "intentional variation."

A wide squat stance isn't wrong. A valgus collapse is.
The same knee inward position means different things in different contexts.

We've had to build judgment, not just detection.
```

```
We geo-price FORM: $9/month Ukraine/EE, $19/month Western EU and US.

Not a discount. Same product. PPP-adjusted because it's the right call.

If someone in Kyiv can't afford a $19/month trainer, charging $19 doesn't
make us more successful — it just excludes people who need it.
```

### Pillar 5: Fitness culture critique

Institutional critique earns authority when it's specific and substantiated. Not "the fitness industry is broken" — everyone says that. Instead: specific products, specific patterns, specific data.

Examples:
```
Most "AI fitness apps" run the same logic:
take a 6-week template, assign it based on goal + experience,
call that "personalization."

I tested 8 of them. The prompts differ. The logic doesn't.

That's not AI coaching. It's a questionnaire with a randomizer.
```

```
Calorie tracking apps that show a red number when you go over.

Red = danger. Failure. Stop.

The behavioral signal is: eating is a performance you're failing.

That's not health. That's a designed relationship with food
that some people will carry for years.
```

Keep these honest: if you're critiquing a competitor's specific design choice, be specific about the choice, not the company. "This feature pattern is harmful" reads as principled. "Company X is terrible" reads as competitive.

## 5 · Weekly cadence

### Volume

**5 posts per week, Monday through Friday.** This is the floor from `docs/CONTENT_CALENDAR.md`. Do not go below it for more than one week — accounts that post intermittently do not build audiences.

Saturday and Sunday: optional. If something genuinely interesting happened (a user interview insight, a decision made), post it. Do not manufacture content for the weekend.

### Weekly structure

| Day | Type | Approximate length |
|---|---|---|
| Monday | Training science take or industry critique | 1 to 3 tweets |
| Tuesday | Build-in-public update (decision, progress, cut) | 1 to 3 tweets |
| Wednesday | Victor voice preview or product pillar | 1 tweet |
| Thursday | Engagement post — respond to something in the fitness community, add a perspective | 1 tweet |
| Friday | "Weekly deep" — one concept expanded into a 4 to 6 tweet thread | Thread |

The Friday thread is the anchor of the week. It should connect to longer-form content (a blog post draft, a research finding, an aspect of the build). These threads drive follower growth more than any other format because they are worth saving and sharing.

### Threads vs. standalone tweets

**Use threads when:** the idea requires more than 280 characters to be honest, or when you want the content to surface as a structured asset (shareable, quotable, findable). The Friday slot is always a thread.

**Use standalone tweets when:** the idea is complete in one statement. Most training science takes and Victor previews are standalone. Do not pad short ideas into threads — it reads as filler.

**Never thread for the sake of threading.** A 14-tweet thread where tweets 7 through 11 are filler is worse than a 5-tweet thread that stops when it's done.

## 6 · Build-in-public formula

### What to share

**Decision rationale.** Not just "we decided X" — explain the considered alternatives and why you chose this one. People follow build-in-public accounts for the thinking, not the announcement.

**Honest milestones.** "We completed the CV pipeline for 8 lifts. Accuracy on squat is 91%. Accuracy on push-up is 78%. We're not happy with push-up. Here's why it's harder."

**Things you cut.** Cutting features is underreported and high-signal. It shows product discipline. "We had a body composition tracking screen. We deleted it. Here's what changed our minds."

**Research findings.** Anonymized, aggregated. "In 12 of 15 interviews, people described the same problem: they have a program, they're just not confident they're executing it correctly." This validates the product without fabricating metrics.

**Genuine uncertainty.** "We haven't figured out how to handle rest days yet. Open question." This is not weakness — it is the kind of honesty that makes people want to follow the resolution.

**The Ukraine context, when relevant.** Not as a constant emotional frame, but when it genuinely affects the build — a power cut, a team decision made under unusual conditions, the reason a certain feature matters to the local market.

### What not to share

**Fabricated or unverifiable metrics.** No "200 users already signed up!" if the number is an estimate or a goal. If you have a waitlist, say how many are on it and when you started collecting. If you don't know, don't cite it.

**Competitor attacks.** Never name a competitor in a disparaging post. Critique patterns, not products. The difference: "apps that use loss aversion mechanics to drive retention" vs. "Fitbod does X which is bad." The first is a principle. The second is petty.

**Hype language.** "Game-changing," "revolutionary," "the app that will change how you train forever." This language signals that you don't trust the product to speak for itself.

**Fake transparency.** Sharing a decision after it's already set and framed positively is not transparency — it's post-hoc marketing dressed up as openness. Real build-in-public means sharing things before the outcome is certain.

**Investor-speak.** "We're building toward X TAM with Y unit economics." Your audience on X is not a board room. If you want to signal business sophistication, show it through the quality of your product decisions, not by using VC vocabulary.

## 7 · Engagement strategy

### Who to follow — categories, not specific accounts

The goal is to be in genuine conversations with the people who are FORM's audience and adjacent experts. Follow accounts in these categories:

**Sports scientists and strength researchers.** People who publish in NSCA, IJSPP, Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, who explain evidence-based training on Twitter. These are the people whose retweets validate FORM's scientific credibility.

**HRV and sleep researchers.** Autonomic nervous system researchers, sleep scientists who comment on athlete recovery. HRV-aware programming is a FORM differentiator — being in those conversations matters.

**Honest strength coaches.** Coaches with real client loads who post about what actually works and what doesn't. Not the transformation-photo coaches — the ones who post about RPE, training age, and deload protocols.

**Fitness app critics and indie developers.** People building or critiquing digital health products. The intersection of product thinking and fitness is where FORM's build-in-public story finds its most engaged audience.

**Ukrainian tech and startup community.** Founders and developers building from UA. This is an authentic part of the story and a community that actively supports Ukrainian products.

Do not follow or engage with: supplement sponsors, transformation challenge accounts, calorie-restriction maximalists, or anyone whose content conflicts with FORM's clinical safety principles.

### How to engage

**Add to the conversation, do not just agree.** A reply that says "great point" has no value. A reply that extends an idea, adds a nuance, or cites a relevant finding is a signal of expertise.

**Disagree when you have grounds to.** Polite, evidence-based disagreement is how accounts get noticed by the people you want to reach. Do not pick fights, but do not perform agreement.

**Reply to fitness questions in the community.** When someone tweets "why is my squat stalling," and you have a real answer grounded in programming principles, give it. No product mention necessary. This is how you build a reputation before the product exists.

**Do not use replies as soft ads.** "Great question — this is exactly what FORM solves!" is immediately off-putting. If you are genuinely helping in a reply, the bio does the work. Let people find the product — do not drag it into every conversation.

**Engage with your own replies promptly.** `docs/CONTENT_CALENDAR.md` sets a target of responding to thoughtful replies within 6 hours. This is achievable pre-launch when volume is low. It matters: people who get a real response become advocates.

## 8 · Beta recruiting via X

X is one of the higher-yield channels for recruiting qualified beta users, particularly for the wearable power-user segment (Артур persona in `docs/RESEARCH_SCREENER.md`). The recruiting flow is detailed in `docs/RESEARCH_SCREENER.md` Section 5 — this section covers the X-specific strategy layer on top of that.

### The sequence that works

**Step 1: Build the audience first.** You cannot recruit from an audience that does not exist. The 60 to 90 days of Phase 1 content is not just marketing — it is the prerequisite for recruiting. People need to see who you are before they will respond to a DM.

**Step 2: Public recruiting posts.** Before using DMs, post publicly about recruiting beta users. Something like:

```
Looking for 25 serious lifters for FORM closed beta — iPhone, trains 3x+ per week,
self-coached or has been coached before.

45-min video call for user research first, then early TestFlight access.
$50 gift card for the research call, no pitch.

If this is you, reply or DM.
```

Post this twice, spaced at least 2 weeks apart, to reach different parts of your audience. Pin it for one week when running active recruiting.

**Step 3: Targeted DM outreach — after engagement.** Use the search operators from `docs/RESEARCH_SCREENER.md` to find people who tweet about their training and wearables. Like and reply to 1 to 2 of their tweets first. Wait at least 24 hours. Then DM with a specific reference to their content.

The rule in `docs/RESEARCH_SCREENER.md` is absolute: never cold-DM without prior engagement. The response rate is below 1% cold. It rises to 5 to 10% after genuine prior engagement.

**Step 4: Track source.** Every beta recruit from X should be tagged in your tracking sheet with source = X and ideally the specific post that converted them. This data tells you which content types drive recruiting conversion, which informs content prioritization.

### What to say and what not to say in DMs

Do say: who you are, what the research call is (45 min, no pitch, paid), what you're building in one sentence, why you thought of them specifically (reference their tweet).

Do not say: how many users you have, what your valuation is, how many investors are interested, that this will "change how they train." None of that helps a stranger decide to give you 45 minutes.

The goal of the DM is one thing: get them to fill out the screener. Not to convince them the product is great.

## 9 · Voice rules for X

Victor's full voice specification lives in `docs/BRAND.md` Section 5 and in the `brand-voice` agent. This section translates that specification specifically to the 280-character format.

### What carries over from Victor's voice to X

**Short sentences win.** Victor uses three words when others use twelve. This is not a stylistic choice — it is the product voice. On Twitter the constraint is structural, which makes it the natural home for Victor-adjacent content.

**Data before adjectives.** "HRV dropped 15%" not "your recovery was really poor." On X, where credibility is thin, specificity is the only substitute for reputation.

**No motivational filler.** Not on launch posts, not on hard days. If there is nothing useful to say, do not post. The cadence goal is 5 posts per week — occasionally posting 4 is better than padding to 5 with "reminder: consistency matters."

**Acknowledge complexity without retreating to vagueness.** "It depends" is not an acceptable answer. "It depends on training age — here's what that means for an intermediate vs. advanced lifter" is.

### X-specific voice rules

**No hashtags in the post body.** Hashtags kill the voice. If you need discoverability, use them in the first reply. FORM's content should not look like it was written by a social media manager in 2019.

**No rhetorical questions as hooks.** "Have you ever wondered why your HRV is inconsistent?" is a bad opening. "HRV inconsistency has four common causes, and most apps show you none of them" is better.

**No "thread incoming."** If you're posting a thread, start the thread. The audience can see it is a thread. Do not announce it.

**No engagement bait.** "What do you think?" and "Reply with your experience below!" are the laziest moves in social media and the ones most inconsistent with Victor's voice. You can invite conversation by leaving a genuine question in the thread — but it should be a real question you don't know the answer to, not a prompt.

**Numbers need context.** "91% accuracy" means nothing without: on what lift, under what conditions, compared to what. On X you have the space to add one line of context. Always add it.

### How X voice differs from other channels

| Channel | Tone | Length | Victor presence |
|---|---|---|---|
| X / Twitter | Direct, takes, fragments | 280 chars / short threads | High — Victor-adjacent |
| Newsletter | Essay, long-form, exploratory | 1500+ words | Moderate — founder voice |
| LinkedIn | Professional narrative, founder story | 300-700 words | Low — George's voice |
| Blog | Research-grade, argued | 1500-3000 words | Low — authoritative |
| Reddit | Helpful, community-native | Variable | None — just expertise |

The mistake to avoid: writing LinkedIn posts and copying them to X. They do not translate. LinkedIn rewards narrative arc and professional relatability. X rewards compression, takes, and intellectual confidence. Write for each separately.

## 10 · Phase 2: Launch week

On the day FORM goes live on the App Store, the primary X asset is the 14-tweet launch thread documented in `content/twitter-launch-thread.md`.

Read that document before launch week. The thread covers: what FORM does, the camera pipeline, HRV-aware programming, Victor's voice, the ED safety design, the Ukraine context, pricing, what is next, and a direct CTA.

### What to do in addition to the thread

**The day before launch:** post a single tweet that signals something is coming without manufactured hype. One line. "Tomorrow." Or: "We submit to the App Store tonight. Thread in the morning." No countdown graphics, no "the wait is almost over."

**The week after the thread:** post daily with real updates.
- T+24h: honest first-day numbers. Installs, top countries, anything surprising.
- T+72h: first feedback themes. What users are saying, what is working, what is not.
- T+7d: a real week-one retrospective. What you expected, what was different, what changes.

**Engage personally on the launch thread.** The last tweet commits to responding to every reply for 24 hours. Do this. It is the highest-return 24 hours of the launch. The people who reply to a launch thread are almost always high-quality early adopters.

**Do not run paid promotion on launch day.** The note in `content/twitter-launch-thread.md` is correct. Launch day is for learning what organic distribution looks like. Paid amplification obscures the signal.

**Do not cross-post the launch thread to LinkedIn.** LinkedIn gets its own launch post — a different format, a different angle, written for that audience. Copying the thread reads as laziness and performs poorly.

## 11 · Metrics

Track these. Review weekly for the first month, then monthly.

### Account health

**Follower count.** Directional only. A 500-person audience of serious lifters is worth more than 5,000 casual followers. Do not optimize for this number.

**Follower growth rate.** If you post consistently and growth is flat or declining, the content is not resonating. This is a diagnostic signal, not a vanity metric.

**Engagement rate.** Likes + replies + retweets divided by impressions. Under 1% is underperforming. 2% to 5% is healthy for a pre-launch account with a niche audience. Above 5% means the content is hitting something.

**Reply quality.** Are the people replying the kind of people who should become users or amplifiers? Read the replies. If the engagement is coming from the wrong audience, the content positioning is off.

### Conversion metrics

**Profile link clicks.** How many people click through to form.coach per week. This connects X activity to waitlist growth. Use UTM parameter: `?utm_source=twitter&utm_medium=bio`.

**Waitlist signups attributed to X.** Track UTM in the waitlist form. This is the number that justifies the time investment.

**Beta research recruits from X.** How many of the 30 user research participants were found through X. Target from `docs/RESEARCH_SCREENER.md`: 7 to 10 (Артур persona specifically).

**Beta applicants from X.** Of the 800+ applications targeted in `docs/BETA_RECRUITING.md`, what proportion trace back to X engagement. UTM: `?utm_source=twitter&utm_medium=organic`.

### Content performance

**Thread saves and retweets.** These indicate content people want to return to or show others. High saves = strong content. Low saves but high likes = entertaining but not substantive.

**Which pillar drives most profile clicks.** If training science threads consistently drive more link clicks than build-in-public posts, weight your content toward training science. Let the data shape the mix.

**Time to first meaningful reply.** If good posts go several hours without responses, your audience is too small or not engaged enough. This is a Phase 1 diagnostic — it should improve as the audience builds.

Do not track: total impressions (too gameable, too easy to inflate with controversy). Do not report follower count as a success metric in investor updates — `docs/MARKETING.md` Section 10 is clear on which vanity metrics not to surface.

## 12 · Honest limitations

X will not solve distribution by itself. Before optimizing deeper into the platform, know what it cannot do.

**X will not scale installs.** The fitness Twitter audience is real but not large. A fully engaged account with 2,000 quality followers might drive 100 to 300 installs at launch. That is meaningful for a closed beta; it is not enough to replace App Store ASO, Reddit, or earned media for a public launch.

**X is not where casual fitness users live.** FORM's core user — a 27-year-old who self-coaches and uses Hevy — may or may not be on Twitter. The people active on fitness Twitter skew toward coaches, researchers, and enthusiasts who follow training science closely. This is the right audience for credibility-building, but it is not a complete picture of the TAM.

**Build-in-public has a shelf life.** It works before PMF because it generates community and feedback. After PMF, as the user base grows, the same transparency that builds credibility in phase 1 can create noise and anxiety in phase 2 (sharing every problem makes existing users nervous). Plan to shift from "building" content toward "what we've learned" and "here is what works" content after public launch.

**Viral moments cannot be engineered.** Do not write content optimized for going viral. Write content that is genuinely useful to serious lifters. The distribution will follow at the rate it follows. Chasing virality produces content that performs well for one day and adds followers who leave when they realize the account is not entertainment.

**The comparison trap.** Other fitness apps and fitness founders have larger X followings. Do not benchmark against them. FORM's audience is narrow by design — serious, self-coached lifters who are genuinely underserved. A smaller, correctly-targeted audience at launch is structurally better than a large, diffuse one.

**When to deprioritize.** If any of the following are true, reduce X activity and redirect time:
- App Store submission deadline is approaching and the build needs attention
- A user research sprint is active and interview synthesis is behind
- A critical product decision needs focused thinking

X is a long-term channel. Missing one week of posts is recoverable. Missing a product decision because you were posting is not.
