# Why "Functional Training" Is a Fuzzy Category

The fitness industry's most durable marketing term is also its most scientifically incoherent one. "Functional training" has been used to justify everything from single-leg BOSU ball squats to kettlebell flows to loaded carries to sled pushes — exercises so mechanically dissimilar that grouping them under one label tells you almost nothing about their actual training stimulus. The problem is not that these exercises are useless. Some of them are excellent. The problem is that "functional" as it is typically marketed describes generality, not specificity. And the foundational principle of training adaptation — the one that has been in the literature since at least the 1950s — demands specificity. These two concepts are in direct tension, and the fitness industry has spent thirty years pretending the tension does not exist.

---

## SAID: The Principle That Makes "General Functional" an Oxymoron

The Specific Adaptation to Imposed Demands principle is the organizing framework of training science. Its core claim is simple: the body adapts to the specific demands placed on it, and those adaptations do not reliably generalize beyond the conditions that produced them. The neuromuscular system becomes more efficient at producing force in specific velocity ranges, joint angles, and stabilization requirements. The musculoskeletal system remodels along lines of mechanical stress that are specific to loading direction, magnitude, and rate. Cardiovascular adaptations differ depending on whether training was continuous, intermittent, heavy, or light.

SAID was not invented to sell supplements. It describes an observed biological reality: stressed biological systems adapt locally and specifically, not globally and generally. This is why a competitive swimmer who has trained for ten years does not walk into a road race and run a competitive 10K. The aerobic capacity is there. The specific neuromuscular recruitment patterns, the running economy, the tendon stiffness at the ankle — these are not.

The implication for "functional training" is immediate and uncomfortable: if an exercise is designed to be generally functional — to train balance, coordination, core stability, and strength all at once in a multi-planar, real-world-mimicking movement — then by definition it cannot be maximally specific to any single one of those adaptations. You are distributing the training stimulus across multiple systems. Each system receives a diluted signal. The adaptation to each is correspondingly smaller than what would occur if you had trained that system with concentrated, specific loading.

This is not a theoretical objection. It has been measured in the laboratory, repeatedly.

## The Instability Evidence: What Force Measures Actually Show

The cleanest experimental manipulation for testing "functional" claims involves unstable surface training — BOSU balls, wobble boards, suspension trainers, and similar devices. The argument for these tools is intuitive: real-world movement requires balance and stabilization, so training on unstable surfaces should build capacity that transfers to real-world performance. Behm and Colado (2012, *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research*) reviewed this claim against the available evidence and found it substantially unsupported.

Their central finding: training on unstable surfaces reduces force output at equivalent or lower levels of muscle activation compared to stable conditions. The force deficit is not compensated by greater total muscle recruitment — it is simply a force reduction. When you squat on a BOSU ball, you move less weight, produce less ground reaction force, and create a smaller mechanical stimulus in the primary movers. The instability does not add training stimulus to the legs. It removes it.

The mechanism is straightforward neuromechanics. Under unstable conditions, the nervous system's first priority is postural maintenance and fall prevention — not maximal force production in the primary movers. Motor unit recruitment in the prime movers is modulated downward to prioritize reactive stabilization. You get less quadriceps development per set, not more.

Behm and Anderson (2006, *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research*) examined trunk muscle activation during stable versus unstable resistance training exercises. Their findings were more nuanced: core musculature — particularly the deep stabilizers including transverse abdominis and multifidus — does show elevated EMG activity under unstable conditions. The instability challenge is real; it is just not where the marketing claims it is. The core gets more stimulus. The legs get less.

Anderson and Behm (2004) extended this with EMG analysis comparing the same exercises under stable and unstable conditions. The pattern held: instability shifted neural demand toward postural muscles and away from the primary movers producing the bulk of force. This is a feature if your goal is postural muscle training. It is a bug if your goal is maximal lower body strength development.

Willardson (2007, *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research*) addressed this by proposing an instability continuum — a framework in which the appropriate level of instability for an exercise is determined by the desired training outcome, the athlete's training history, and the specificity of the target sport. The continuum is useful precisely because it treats instability as a variable with specific applications, not a universal improvement over stable training. At one end of the continuum are exercises requiring maximal stability (heavy barbell squats, deadlifts, bench press on a firm bench) — appropriate when the goal is maximal force production and hypertrophy. At the other end are exercises with high instability challenge — appropriate for specific rehabilitation and sport contexts that Willardson specified carefully and narrowly.

## Transfer of Training: The Specificity Paradox in Practice

The specificity paradox can be stated precisely: the more generalized an exercise — the more "functional" in the industry's sense — the less specific its training stimulus, and therefore the weaker its transfer to any particular athletic or physical performance outcome.

Barnett, Ross, Lord, and Thomas (2002) examined the transfer-of-training evidence directly. Their review reached a conclusion that has not been sufficiently absorbed by the training industry: general training does not reliably transfer to specific performance, and specific training does not reliably transfer to general capacity. Transfer of training is consistently weaker than practitioners assume, in both directions. This finding has significant implications for how exercise selection should be justified.

If you train an athlete with "functional" multi-planar, unstable, complex movement patterns in the gym, the direct performance transfer to their sport skill is not guaranteed and is often small. The body adapts to the gym exercise, not to the sport skill. If you want an athlete to improve at a specific sporting movement, you need to train that movement or its closest mechanical approximation. The SAID principle demands it. Barnett et al. (2002) documented that it plays out that way empirically.

The counterargument — that general physical preparedness underlies all specific performance — is partially correct but often overstated. GPP matters most in novice athletes whose general physical capacity is the limiting factor. As training age increases, the limiting factors become increasingly specific, and the performance gains from general training diminish accordingly. An elite cross-country skier's performance is not meaningfully constrained by whether they can balance on a wobble board. It is constrained by their VO2max, lactate threshold, and double-pole power at race velocity.

## The Rehabilitation Exception: Where Instability Training Has Real Evidence

There is a genuine, research-supported application for unstable surface training, and it is not what most gym-goers are doing it for.

Post-injury proprioceptive rehabilitation is the legitimate domain. Following ankle sprain, ACL reconstruction, or other joint injuries that damage mechanoreceptors and disrupt afferent proprioceptive signaling, progressive exposure to controlled instability challenges the regenerating sensory pathways and helps restore neuromuscular control at the joint. This is a specific rehabilitation goal with a specific mechanism, and unstable surface training is appropriately targeted to it.

Kibler, Press, and Sciascia (2006, *Sports Medicine*) made a critical distinction in their review of core stability in athletic function. They separated "functional" movement as used in rehabilitation — where the goal is restoring normal movement mechanics and proprioceptive integrity — from "functional" training as used in general performance enhancement. The rehabilitation context is addressing a specific deficit: impaired joint mechanoreception and neuromuscular coordination. The training context is often addressing no specific deficit at all — or worse, assuming that because a movement "looks functional," it must train the thing it looks like it trains.

This distinction collapses in most gym and group fitness environments. An elite soccer player twelve weeks post-ACL reconstruction doing proprioceptive balance training is engaged in an evidence-based intervention. A recreationally active person with no injury history doing BOSU ball squats because they heard it "activates more stabilizers" is engaged in reduced-force-output training with weaker primary mover stimulus and a marginal stabilizer benefit that could be achieved more efficiently with other means.

## Sport Contexts Where the Instability Argument Has Merit

Not every performance context is a stable environment. The specificity argument cuts both ways: if your sport actually requires high-level neuromuscular control under unstable conditions, then training under unstable conditions is specific, not general.

Skiing, both alpine and cross-country, involves constant reactive balance demands on shifting terrain. Surfing requires millisecond-level balance adjustments on a dynamic surface. Wrestling and grappling require strength production during states of compromised base of support — an opponent actively destabilizing your position. Gymnastics, equestrian sport, and stand-up paddleboarding all involve sustained performance on surfaces that move independently of the athlete.

For athletes in these sports, progressive exposure to controlled instability during training is justified by SAID. The unstable surface is not an approximation of a stable sport environment; it is an approximation of the actual sport environment. The specificity argument that condemns BOSU ball squats for a recreational lifter is the same argument that supports proprioceptive training for a slalom skier. The principle is consistent — it is the application context that changes.

The key word is "controlled." Specificity-justified instability training should resemble the actual demands of the sport in terms of the type of instability, the joint positions involved, and the force-velocity profile required. Random instability — a wobble board because it feels challenging — is not the same as specifically designed reactive training matching the balance demands of slalom gate skiing. The distinction matters for program design.

## What "Core Strength" Claims Get Wrong

Perhaps the most persistent claim in functional training marketing is that unstable surface exercises dramatically increase core muscle activation, justifying their use for everyone. The evidence here is more complicated than either side usually admits.

The Behm and Anderson (2006) data do show elevated deep trunk muscle activation under unstable conditions. This is not fabricated. But elevated EMG activity in a muscle is not the same as an effective training stimulus for that muscle. Training adaptation requires sufficient mechanical load, sufficient metabolic stress, and sufficient mechanical tension on the muscle fibers. Heightened activation in a muscle bearing very little load — which is the condition of the core under unstable resistance training — produces a much weaker adaptive stimulus than lower activation under substantial load.

Heavy compound lifts under stable conditions — squats, deadlifts, barbell rows, overhead press — produce substantial core muscle activation and, critically, do so while the primary movers are also under high load. There is no conflict between core recruitment and force production in these movements. The core is trained as part of a high-load stimulus, not as a consolation prize after force output has been reduced to preserve postural control.

For most people who want a stronger, more functional core, the evidence supports training under progressive load rather than progressive instability. Loaded carries — farmer's walks, suitcase carries, Zercher carries — produce sustained trunk muscle activation under substantial load in movement contexts that do genuinely transfer to carrying, lifting, and locomotion demands. These are far less photogenic than BOSU ball exercises and correspondingly underrepresented in fitness marketing.

## Why the Industry Gets This Backwards

The fitness industry's incentive structure explains the persistence of functional training mythology. Novel-looking exercises are more marketable than heavy squats. Equipment sales depend on BOSU balls and wobble boards and suspension systems that create the appearance of complexity. Group fitness formats require simultaneous instruction of people with vastly different training ages, which pushes toward lower loads and higher novelty. Certification courses that can be completed in a weekend need concepts that are intuitively appealing without requiring deep technical knowledge — and "train movements, not muscles" and "challenge your stabilizers" are both intuitively appealing and technically shallow.

The SAID principle provides no such marketing advantage. It is not a brand. It does not require proprietary equipment. It suggests that effective training is often less visually impressive than ineffective training — a heavy barbell squat does not look as interesting as a single-leg BOSU squat with a cable rotation, but it produces a larger training stimulus per minute by nearly every relevant measure for a non-injured recreational lifter.

## The Honest Integration

The honest position on functional training is not that stability training is useless. It is that "functional" is doing too much work as a descriptor, and that work is obscuring the specificity questions that should actually drive exercise selection.

Every exercise is specific to something. A BOSU ball squat is specific to BOSU ball squatting — it improves balance on a BOSU ball, develops postural muscle co-activation at low loads, and trains the skill of that particular movement pattern. A heavy bilateral squat is specific to heavy bilateral squatting — it develops maximal lower body force production, hypertrophies the quadriceps, hamstrings, and glutes under high mechanical tension, and trains the neuromuscular skill of that pattern. Neither is intrinsically more "functional." The question is: which is more specific to your training goal?

For most recreational lifters seeking strength, hypertrophy, or general physical capacity, the answer is almost always the stable, loaded compound movement. The SAID principle demands concentrated, specific stimulus. Diluting that stimulus across multiple systems simultaneously — which is what truly "functional" multi-modal exercises do — produces weaker adaptation in each target system.

For post-injury rehabilitation, the balance of evidence supports progressive instability challenges targeting the injured joint's proprioceptive system. For athletes in balance-dependent sports, the evidence supports sport-specific instability training that closely mimics the actual neuromuscular demands of competition.

That is the entire case for instability training, stated honestly. It is narrower than the marketing suggests, better-grounded than the critics allow, and impossible to state without reference to training specificity — which means it is impossible to state without reference to the SAID principle that functional training mythology is perpetually at odds with.

The research does not support the BOSU ball as a universal training upgrade. It supports specificity as a universal training principle. These are not the same thing.
