## Your wearable has an opinion. It's not always right.

You've spent six weeks building a habit of checking your readiness score before you train.

Monday: 87. Train hard. You do. Good session.

Tuesday: 54. Take it easy. You scale back. Fine.

Friday: 42. Your device tells you your recovery is poor. You look at the programme — it's the heavy squat day you've been building toward for three weeks. You sit it out.

Two weeks later, same situation. 44. Same session. You sit it out again.

At some point you notice: you haven't done a heavy squat day in a month. Not because of what your body was doing. Because of what the device said.

This is the trap. And it's not the device's fault.

---

**The problem isn't the technology**

Consumer wearables are genuinely useful. Optical heart rate monitoring works well enough for resting HR and broad trends. Sleep staging gives you something to look at. HRV trends over weeks can signal cumulative stress. These are real signals, not nothing.

The problem is the gap between *signal* and *prescription*.

Your wearable measures photoplethysmography — light bouncing off blood vessels in your wrist. From that signal, it infers heart rate variability. From HRV, it infers recovery. From recovery, it outputs a readiness score. Each step in that chain involves assumptions, population averages, and a model that was not built from your training history.

A consumer-grade device estimating HRV from a PPG sensor at the wrist has meaningful accuracy limits compared to chest-strap ECG measurement — particularly during sleep, where the gap widens. This isn't a reason to throw the device away. It's a reason to hold the output loosely.

---

**HRV: trend is signal, daily point is noise**

HRV is legitimately useful for tracking training readiness. The evidence base is real. Elite athletes and sports scientists use it routinely.

The part that often gets lost in consumer wearable marketing: the useful signal is the *trend over 7–14 days*, not the daily number.

A single HRV reading varies based on how you positioned your arm, when exactly you woke up, what you drank the night before, what time of day it was measured. The noise floor on a daily point is substantial. Looking at your HRV trend over two weeks — whether it's drifting down, stable, or climbing — is a different (more useful) exercise than checking the number before each session.

Most readiness scores compress this trend into a daily binary: green or red. That compression loses information. Sometimes red on Monday is noise. Sometimes a week of yellow is the signal everyone's missing.

---

**What your training log has that your device doesn't**

Your device knows your heart rate and movement. It does not know:

- Whether last week was a deliberate accumulation phase or a mistake
- Whether the fatigue you feel is adaptation in progress or overreaching
- Whether this specific session at this specific weight is one you've done before and handled
- What your sleep looked like *before* the wearable — and whether this is normal for you

Your training log has this information. Over months, it builds a picture of your personal dose-response that no population-average model can replicate.

When there's a conflict between a readiness score and what you know about your training history, your training log usually deserves more weight — not less.

---

**Three practical boundaries**

Where wearables are useful:

**1. Chronic load monitoring.** If your HRV trend has drifted down for 10–14 days and subjective fatigue tracks it, that's a real signal. Time to look at whether the programme needs a deload.

**2. Sleep trend, not sleep staging.** Total time in bed, broad consistency, major disruptions — useful. The exact percentage of deep sleep a wrist sensor inferred from movement and heart rate — hold that loosely.

**3. As one input, not the input.** The device contributes information. Your feel in the warmup, your bar speed on working sets, your training log — these contribute information too. The job is integration, not delegation.

Where the delegation breaks down:

When the device replaces subjective assessment instead of supplementing it. When green means "train hard regardless of feel" and red means "skip regardless of everything else". When you spend more time optimising your readiness score than you spend improving your training.

---

**The training log is primary**

We've spent a lot of this newsletter block talking about research — what it shows, what it can't show, how to read it. The same principle applies to wearable data: it's information about a population model applied to you, not information about you.

The instrument that actually records *your* specific dose-response, your personal history, the exact sessions that drove adaptation and the ones that didn't — that's your training log. Every bit of external data (research findings, readiness scores, app recommendations) is an input to that record. Not a replacement for it.

Your device is a useful tool. It's not the coach.
