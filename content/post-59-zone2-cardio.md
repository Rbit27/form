Zone 2 has become the default prescription for anyone who asks about cardiovascular training. Three hours per week. Nose breathing. 180 minus your age. The MAF method. You can hold a conversation but it is not comfortable. Every coach on the internet has adopted the language, and most have quietly dropped the caveats that make it coherent.

Here is the evidence base. And here is who it was actually collected on.

## What Zone 2 Is, Physiologically

Zone 2 corresponds roughly to training at or just below the first lactate threshold (LT1) — the intensity at which blood lactate begins to accumulate above resting levels, typically around 1.5–2 mmol/L. Below this threshold, oxidative metabolism dominates: your aerobic system clears lactate as fast as it is produced. Above it, clearance begins to lag, and the work of managing that accumulation has downstream consequences for subsequent fatigue and recovery.

The physiological zone is real. The training adaptations below LT1 are real: mitochondrial biogenesis via PGC-1α upregulation, increased capillary density, improved fat oxidation at submaximal intensities, enhanced cardiac stroke volume. Holloszy (1967) established the mitochondrial biogenesis foundation. Hood et al. (2000) extended the PGC-1α mechanism. None of this is contested.

What is contested is the prescription — specifically, how much of it non-endurance athletes need, and what it does for people whose primary training is not endurance-based.

## Where the 80/20 Model Comes From

The polarized training framework — roughly 80% of training volume below LT1, 20% at or above LT2 — comes from Stephen Seiler's work analyzing training distribution in elite endurance athletes (Seiler & Kjerland, 2006; Seiler, 2010). The finding was that high-level cross-country skiers, rowers, cyclists, and distance runners spent the majority of their training at low intensity, with a meaningful block at high intensity, and relatively little in the middle.

This has been extrapolated into a universal prescription. That extrapolation is the problem.

Seiler's subjects were training 10–15+ hours per week. They were professional or sub-elite endurance athletes. The 80/20 distribution emerged from a structural reality of high-volume training: if you are doing 12 hours per week of running or cycling, you cannot sustain that volume at high intensity. You train easy because you have to, and you train hard periodically because adaptation requires it.

The elite endurance athlete training at volume has a structural reason to do 80% Zone 2. The person doing 6 hours per week of strength training does not.

## Mitochondrial Adaptation Without the Label

Here is the inconvenient middle: your strength training program is already generating meaningful mitochondrial adaptation, and the low-intensity movement you do outside the gym — walking, cycling to work, daily activity — contributes to the aerobic base that Seiler's framework assumes is being deliberately built.

Resistance training increases PGC-1α expression (Timmons et al., 2005). High-intensity interval training drives mitochondrial biogenesis as effectively as continuous moderate-intensity training in significantly less time (Gibala et al., 2006; Burgomaster et al., 2008). Neither of these is Zone 2. Both produce the adaptation Zone 2 is credited with.

The efficiency question matters if your training time is finite. If you have 30 minutes for a cardiovascular session, the evidence for HIIT producing equivalent or superior mitochondrial stimulus in less time is robust. The assumption that Zone 2 volume is the superior path is not robustly supported for time-constrained athletes whose primary sport is not endurance.

The people for whom Zone 2 volume is unambiguously optimal are the people who need to train for hours every day without destroying their recovery capacity. That is a specific population with specific structural constraints.

## The Interference Research

For strength-focused athletes, the relevant literature is the concurrent training research — whether cardiovascular training undermines strength and hypertrophy adaptations.

Wilson et al. (2012) published the most comprehensive meta-analysis on concurrent training. The headline: concurrent training attenuates strength and hypertrophy gains compared to strength training alone, with the interference effect increasing with frequency, duration, and intensity of cardiovascular work. Critically, mode matters: running produced greater interference than cycling. Low-frequency, moderate-duration work produced less interference than high-frequency, long-duration work.

This does not argue against cardiovascular training for strength athletes. It argues for precision in dosing. A 30-minute cycling session twice per week produces different results than 5 days of 60-minute running. The interference effect is dose-dependent.

Zone 2 done correctly — low-interference mode (cycling, rowing), moderate frequency, positioned away from primary strength sessions — does not meaningfully compromise hypertrophy or strength development. Zone 2 done carelessly is five days of jogging that eats into recovery capacity and attenuates your mass gains for minimal return.

## What Zone 2 Is Actually Useful For

Be specific about the use cases.

**Cardiovascular disease risk reduction.** The evidence for moderate-intensity continuous training improving VO2max, insulin sensitivity, and cardiac health is strong and consistent across populations. If health span is part of your goal, not just performance, Zone 2 has clear support independent of any performance argument.

**Work capacity for longer training sessions.** If your sessions run 75+ minutes and you are fading before completing programmed volume, a higher aerobic base helps. Improved fat oxidation at submaximal intensities means less reliance on glycolytic pathways in the earlier portions of long sessions. If you are gassing out on your fourth working set of squats, this is a relevant variable.

**Active recovery.** True Zone 2 — genuinely low intensity, blood lactate under 2 mmol/L — can facilitate recovery between sessions through increased blood flow without adding meaningful metabolic stress. The operative word is "true." Most people performing what they believe is Zone 2 are operating above LT1 and adding a moderate-intensity training stimulus, not a recovery session.

**General physical preparedness for advanced athletes.** For athletes who have been strength training seriously for years, the aerobic floor matters for total fitness, not just the strength ceiling. The argument for Zone 2 gets stronger as the athlete becomes more complete.

## What It Is Not

Zone 2 is not a fat-loss mechanism that outperforms a caloric deficit. The "fat-burning zone" framing conflates fuel source with total caloric expenditure. More fat burned per minute of exercise does not mean more fat lost over time if total energy expenditure is lower than higher-intensity work.

Zone 2 is not a replacement for the quality of your strength programming. A weak program with Zone 2 appended is a weak program with Zone 2 appended.

Zone 2 is not a universal prescription for everyone who exercises. The dose is what makes it useful or pointless. For someone doing four strength sessions per week with two Zone 2 sessions at genuine low intensity, the Zone 2 is not the variable driving progress. The strength training is. The Zone 2 is insurance — and insurance is worth having, but it is not the thing you are building.

## What FORM Tracks Here

The cardiovascular data FORM uses — resting heart rate trend, HRV baseline, workout heart rate response over time — gives a proxy read on aerobic base without requiring you to do a formal lactate test. If resting HR is trending down over a training block and HRV is stable or improving, the aerobic system is adapting. If it is not, and your sessions are long, that is a variable worth addressing.

The recommendation is not Zone 2 for everyone. It is specificity: know what you are adding, know why, and dose it to complement rather than undermine the training that is actually building your capacity.

---

> **What this means in your next session**
>
> - If you are strength-focused with limited training time, Zone 2 is not your first priority. Your strength sessions and daily movement are generating aerobic adaptation. If you have 30 extra minutes, HIIT produces equivalent mitochondrial stimulus in less time.
> - If you want to add cardiovascular work for health or work capacity, cycling or rowing in low-interference mode twice per week is the right add. Thirty minutes is sufficient; sixty minutes is not necessary.
> - Position cardiovascular work away from your hardest strength sessions — ideally separate days, or post-session on lower-intensity lifting days.
> - If your sessions consistently run long and you fade before completing programmed volume, improving your aerobic base is worth the investment. That is a legitimate Zone 2 application.
> - Log your Zone 2 intensity with actual data: heart rate at perceived exertion, whether you can maintain nasal breathing throughout. "Comfortable" is not a training zone. It is a feeling. Without data, you are probably not where you think you are.
