## Building Victor: What it takes to design an AI coaching voice

The first fitness app I used after my gym sessions ended had one thing to say every morning.

"Great job yesterday! Ready to crush today?"

I hadn't trained the day before. I'd been on a bus for six hours.

It didn't matter. The app didn't know. The app didn't care. The app was not a coach — it was a slot machine that paid out encouragement on a fixed schedule, regardless of what was actually happening with my body, my sleep, or my week.

That's the problem we've been trying to solve. And it's harder than it sounds.

---

**The two bad defaults**

When you try to give AI a coaching voice, you tend to land in one of two places.

The first is the drill sergeant. Short sentences, imperatives, aggression as motivation. It performs discipline. Real coaches who've been in gyms for twenty years are almost never like this. Aggression is a signal they save for moments that call for it. Using it constantly is the coaching equivalent of crying wolf.

The second is the cheerleader. Relentlessly positive. Every set is "amazing." Every session is "incredible work." You're always "crushing it." This voice feels supportive for about three days. Then it becomes wallpaper. You stop reading it. You definitely stop trusting it. If everything is praise, nothing is information.

Victor is neither.

---

**The question we started with**

We didn't start with "what should Victor say?" We started with: what does a coach with twenty years in the trade actually sound like?

I spent weeks listening. Coaches in gyms. Athletes describing their coaches. Interview transcripts. The patterns were consistent.

Real coaches use short sentences. Three to seven words is the norm, not the exception. They lead with data before verdict — they tell you what they observed, then they name what it means. They offer choices rather than orders, especially when something needs to adjust. They stay quiet between sets. They save specific praise for specific moments. Generic praise they almost never use.

That's the template Victor is built from.

---

**The phrase that unlocked it**

There's a Ukrainian saying in training circles: *не пропускай два поспіль* — don't miss two in a row.

One missed session is life. Two in a row is a pattern.

When I landed on this phrase, something clicked. Because it encodes an entire coaching philosophy in six words. It doesn't shame you for missing. It doesn't celebrate you for showing up. It just draws the line that matters — the one between an off day and a drift.

That's Victor's job. Not surveillance. Not cheerleading. Holding the line that actually matters, without drama.

If you miss a session, Victor doesn't say "it's okay, you'll get back on track!" He doesn't say "you let yourself down." He says: the next one matters. Here's what it looks like.

---

**What this looks like in practice**

A competitor-style app, when you skip a workout:

> "Missed yesterday? No worries — you've got this! Let's make today count. Your streak is waiting."

Victor, same situation:

> "Yesterday skipped. Today: 40 min, same lifts, reduced load. Or move to tomorrow if the week's already gone sideways — which day works?"

One of those is a notification. The other is a coaching decision.

The difference isn't warmth versus cold. Victor can be warm. The difference is that Victor is responding to what's actually happening — he acknowledges the skip, makes a concrete offer, gives you agency, and moves on. No streak guilt. No manufactured urgency. No you.

---

**Clinical-safety as a design constraint**

Early in building Victor, I wrote a rule for the team that sounds obvious but turned out to need constant enforcement:

*If you can't say it to a friend at the gym without it feeling strange — Victor doesn't say it.*

That rule mostly catches things involving food, body, and comparison. Victor doesn't comment on what you ate. He doesn't make the connection between training and appearance. He doesn't reference your body as a problem to be solved or a project to be completed.

This isn't a moral stance we're announcing. It's just what a good coach sounds like. A physio doesn't say "let's burn those extra calories." A serious strength coach doesn't comment on how you look. They work with what your body is doing today — not what they think it should look like.

Every piece of copy that touches food, body, injury, or mental state goes through a clinical-safety review before it's published. That includes these newsletters.
