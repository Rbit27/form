# Minimum Effective Dose: What Training Volume You Can Actually Drop

Life compresses. The 5-day-a-week athlete becomes a 2-day-a-week athlete. Kids, job change, a move — the schedule that fit two years ago doesn't fit now.

The question isn't whether to train less. It's what you lose, what you keep, and what the actual floor looks like.

The answer is more forgiving than most assume — and more specific than "just do something."

---

## The Ralston Problem

In 2017, Ralston and colleagues published a meta-analysis in *JSCR* that still reads as counterintuitive to anyone who has spent years optimizing volume. Their finding: **a single set taken to muscular failure produced statistically equivalent strength gains to 3+ sets in untrained and minimally trained individuals.**

This is not a fringe result. The effect size difference was small enough to fall within measurement noise. One set. Significant effort. Comparable outcome — at least at the beginning.

The mechanism isn't mysterious. An untrained nervous system is so far from its adaptive ceiling that a single high-quality stimulus is sufficient to drive the ceiling upward. The system doesn't need repeated stimulation to respond. It needs novelty and sufficient intensity.

The implication everyone misses: **Ralston's finding does not apply to you** if you've been training seriously for more than a year.

The paper is about beginners. The reason it matters for experienced athletes is what it reveals about the *nature* of volume: more is better only until you've already done enough.

---

## MEV Is Not Zero

The practical framework that shapes modern programming — MEV (Minimum Effective Volume), MAV (Maximum Adaptive Volume), MRV (Maximum Recoverable Volume) — treats MEV not as "the fewest sets you can do and survive" but as **the lowest volume that produces positive adaptation in a trained state.**

MEV for a trained individual is not 1 set. It's typically:

- **2–4 sets per muscle group per week** for maintenance of existing muscle mass
- **4–6 sets per muscle group per week** for slow continued growth in an intermediate
- **6–10 sets per muscle group per week** for meaningful hypertrophy stimulus in advanced athletes

These are not precise prescriptions — they're ranges derived from meta-analytic work by Krieger (2010, *JSCR*) and Schoenfeld et al. (2017, *JSCR*), which showed a clear dose-response relationship for hypertrophy in trained individuals. Unlike in Ralston's beginners, adding sets beyond 1 produced meaningfully greater hypertrophy — up to the MAV ceiling, beyond which recovery was compromised.

The key number: **MEV ≈ maintenance volume**. If you drop to MEV, you are not growing. You are preserving what you have. This distinction matters for planning.

## What 3 Hours Per Week Actually Allows

Three hours per week is enough to maintain or slightly grow if distributed intelligently. It is not enough to optimize hypertrophy. These are different objectives and require different mindsets.

Three hours, distributed across 3 days, at 60 minutes per session:

| Day | Focus | Approximate volume |
|---|---|---|
| Monday | Upper push + pull | 6–8 working sets |
| Wednesday | Lower body | 6–8 working sets |
| Friday | Upper push + pull (variation) | 6–8 working sets |

Total weekly sets per major muscle group: 4–8 sets, depending on exercise selection. This is solidly in the MEV-to-low-MAV range. It is maintenance-with-slight-upside, not growth optimization.

Two days at 90 minutes:

| Day | Focus | Approximate volume |
|---|---|---|
| Tuesday | Full body (compound focus) | 8–10 working sets |
| Friday | Full body (variation / accessory emphasis) | 8–10 working sets |

Total weekly sets per major muscle group: 4–6 sets. This is MEV territory. Maintenance is realistic. Slow growth is possible if intensity is high and the exercises are well-chosen. Rapid hypertrophy is not.

## The Frequency Question

One of the most persistent myths in reduced-volume training: that frequency is the key lever.

It is not — not at the MEV end.

Rønnestad and Mujika (2014, *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports*) reviewed the minimum frequency required to maintain strength and muscle mass in trained athletes during periods of reduced training. The data showed:

- **Once per week** at sufficient intensity maintained strength and lean mass for up to 12 weeks in athletes who had previously trained 3–5x/week
- **Twice per week** was meaningfully better for hypertrophy retention
- Frequency mattered less than **total weekly volume** and **whether sets were taken close to failure**

Hickson et al. (1981) is the foundational detraining study here: trained subjects who reduced frequency but kept intensity showed minimal detraining over 10–15 weeks. Those who kept frequency but dropped intensity lost substantially more.

**The practical conclusion:** if you can only do two sessions per week, don't fragment them into low-effort full-body circuits. Concentrate volume, train hard, and keep intensity high. One quality session beats two mediocre ones.

## What Actually Disappears at Reduced Volume

When you drop from 5 sessions per week to 2, several things happen on different timelines:

**Disappears quickly (1–3 weeks):**
- Glycogen-driven "fullness" — this is water and glycogen, not tissue
- Acute exercise-induced cardiovascular adaptation markers
- The feeling of being in "training shape" — partly fatigue clearance, not loss

**Disappears slowly (4–12 weeks at 0 stimulus, much slower with MEV):**
- Myofibrillar hypertrophy — months to meaningfully decline if MEV is maintained
- Maximal strength — decays slowly; Issurin's RTE data puts maximal strength residual at ~30 days with zero training
- Connective tissue adaptations — among the slowest to reverse

**Doesn't disappear within any normal reduction period:**
- Motor patterns — squat mechanics, pull patterns, movement efficiency are deeply encoded
- "Muscle memory" — nuclei added from a previous training period appear to persist (Egner et al., 2013, *PLOS ONE* myonuclei permanence)
- Aerobic base built over years — declines slowly with minimal training stimulus

The myonuclei permanence finding deserves emphasis. Bjørnsen and colleagues (2019, *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*) demonstrated that transient nuclei accumulation following a training phase persists even after detraining. When you return to high volume, regrowth is faster because the cellular machinery is already in place. You are not starting over. You are re-expressing.

## The Intensity Floor

One variable is non-negotiable at reduced volume: **proximity to failure**.

At MEV-level volume, your adaptation signal is concentrated in fewer sets. If those sets are easy, the signal is insufficient and you get neither growth nor maintenance. You drift.

The target: **all working sets at RPE 7–9** (1–3 RIR). At least one set per exercise taken to technical failure or very close to it. No "just going through the motions" when volume is already at the floor.

This is where time constraints most often fail athletes. The reduction in training days encourages a reduction in intensity — longer rest periods, shorter sessions with fewer hard sets, more focus on logistics than on work quality. The schedule compressed but the effort level dropped with it.

The minimum effective dose isn't just a volume number. It's a volume-at-sufficient-intensity number. 4 sets at 40% 1RM is not MEV. 4 hard sets at RPE 8 is.

## The Honest Summary

| Scenario | Realistic Outcome |
|---|---|
| 3× week, 60 min, MEV volume, high intensity | Slow maintenance; possible slight growth in lagging muscle groups |
| 2× week, 90 min, MEV volume, high intensity | Maintenance of muscle mass and strength for 3–6 months reliably |
| 2× week, 60 min, varied intensity, no structure | Gradual drift; not enough stimulus for maintenance |
| 1× week, high intensity, full body | Strength retention likely; hypertrophy retention uncertain beyond 12 weeks |

None of these outcomes is failure. Maintenance at MEV during a high-life-load period is a successful outcome. The goal isn't to grow year-round. The goal is to not regress, stay healthy, and be in position to resume higher volume when life allows.

Training less doesn't undo years of work. It pauses the trajectory. The work remains in the system longer than most people believe.
