# Training in Hot and Cold Environments: Thermoadaptation, CWI, and the Adaptation Tradeoff

Most athletes treat temperature as background noise. You train when it is hot because it is summer. You train when it is cold because the gym is cold. You jump in an ice bath because everyone else is doing it. You occasionally use the sauna because it feels good.

None of this is programmed. None of it is intentional. And in one specific case — cold water immersion during a strength block — it is probably costing you measurable gains.

Temperature is a training variable. This post covers what the evidence actually says: where heat helps performance, where it hurts it, how deliberate heat acclimation works and why it matters for strength athletes, and why cold water immersion is the most practically consequential temperature mistake most lifters are making right now.

---

## Heat: What It Does to Muscle Performance

Temperature's effect on muscle contractile properties is direct and well-characterized. The relationship is not linear in the direction most people assume.

At moderate elevations — muscle temperature in the range of roughly 35–39°C — contractile force and power output improve. The mechanism is thermodynamic: enzymatic reaction rates (ATP hydrolysis, cross-bridge cycling, calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum) speed up with temperature, a phenomenon described by the Q10 coefficient. Viscosity of muscle tissue decreases. Connective tissue becomes more extensible. The result is faster force production, better rate of force development, and reduced injury risk — which is the physiological basis for why a warm-up works at all.

Racinais and Oksa (2010), reviewing the effect of temperature on skeletal muscle contractile properties in the *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine and Science in Sports*, documented that peak force and power output are significantly reduced in cold muscle, and that optimal contractile conditions sit in the elevated but not extreme range of muscle temperature. The physiology is unambiguous here.

Above that range, performance deteriorates through a different mechanism. When core temperature approaches and exceeds 39°C, central fatigue dominates.

**Central fatigue from hyperthermia** is not peripheral — it is not about the muscle running out of fuel or accumulating metabolites. Nybo and Nielsen (2001) demonstrated that at core temperatures above 39–40°C, subjects showed progressive decline in force output that was largely explained by reduced central motor drive, not peripheral muscle failure. A follow-up review by Nybo et al. (2010) in *Journal of Applied Physiology* consolidated the evidence: at high core temperatures, the brain reduces the motor command signal. This is a protective mechanism, not a weakness, but from a performance standpoint the result is motor unit recruitment failure — you lose the ability to fully activate the muscle before the muscle itself is the limiting factor.

**The summary:** Warmth up to approximately 35–39°C muscle temperature is beneficial for contractile performance. Continued heat accumulation driving core temperature above 39°C becomes progressively performance-limiting through central fatigue mechanisms, not peripheral ones. The distinction matters: the gym being warm is different from training to the point of significant core temperature elevation in extreme heat.

Note: heat illness — heat exhaustion, heat stroke — is a medical emergency, not a training variable. If you or someone you are training with develops confusion, stops sweating in extreme heat, or has rapidly elevated core temperature, this requires immediate medical assessment. This post addresses performance physiology, not medical treatment.

**Bottom line: Moderate muscle temperature elevation improves contractile performance. Core temperature above 39°C impairs it through central fatigue. Hot gym ≠ heat illness, but extreme heat training requires managing core temp, not ignoring it.**

## Heat Acclimation: The Protocol That Works

Heat acclimation is not the same as training in a hot gym. It is a deliberate physiological preparation that produces specific, measurable cardiovascular adaptations — and those adaptations are useful for strength athletes even in temperate competition environments.

The primary adaptation is **plasma volume expansion**. Nielsen et al. (1993) documented plasma volume increases of approximately 4–10% following structured heat acclimation protocols. This expansion increases cardiac stroke volume, meaning the heart delivers more blood per beat at the same heart rate. The downstream effects: lower heart rate at any given submaximal workload, reduced perceived exertion at moderate intensities, and improved capacity to sustain effort before cardiovascular strain becomes the limiting factor.

For strength athletes, that last point is directly relevant. During high-volume resistance training sessions — the accumulation blocks where hypertrophy volume is high and rest periods are moderate — cardiovascular strain between sets is a real limiting factor for session quality. Athletes who are cardiovascularly strained are taking longer between sets, reducing total volume, or cutting sets short. Anything that increases cardiovascular capacity at submaximal workloads frees up more capacity for the actual training stimulus.

A standard heat acclimation protocol runs approximately 10 days. The typical format involves 30–60 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise in a heated environment (typically 35–40°C ambient), allowing core temperature to elevate and the body to adapt progressively. By day 5–7, heart rate and core temperature responses to the same workload are measurably reduced. The full cardiovascular adaptation set is reasonably complete by day 10–14.

**Passive heat acclimation through sauna** produces some overlapping adaptations — plasma volume expansion, heat shock protein upregulation, reduced resting heart rate over time — though the evidence for matching the full exercise-heat acclimation effect is weaker. Sauna-based protocols require longer duration (typically 4–8 weeks of regular sessions) to produce comparable cardiovascular adaptations. It is a viable option for athletes whose training context does not allow exercise in hot environments.

**The distinction that matters:** Deliberate heat acclimation is preparation for competition in hot conditions or a specific physiological investment in cardiovascular adaptation. Training in a hot gym because the gym is hot is not heat acclimation — it is training in suboptimal conditions unless structured for that purpose.

**Bottom line: A 10-day exercise-heat acclimation protocol produces plasma volume expansion and cardiovascular efficiency improvements that are relevant for high-volume strength training, not just endurance sport. Passive sauna protocols achieve partially overlapping adaptations over longer timelines.**

## Cold Water Immersion and Strength Training: The Adaptation Tradeoff

This is the most practically important section of this post, and the one most athletes get wrong.

Cold water immersion (CWI) post-training is one of the most widespread recovery practices in strength sport. The rationale is straightforward: CWI reduces muscle soreness, decreases perceived fatigue, and allows athletes to feel better for the next session. All of this is true in the short term.

The problem is what CWI does to the signalling environment that drives adaptation.

The inflammation triggered by a hard resistance training session is not purely a nuisance. It is part of the adaptation signal. Muscle damage activates satellite cells — the stem cells that contribute to muscle repair and hypertrophy. It upregulates mTOR and downstream effectors including p70S6K, which are central to muscle protein synthesis. The acute inflammatory response is part of how the body reads "this tissue was stressed and needs to be stronger."

Cold water immersion suppresses that response.

**Roberts et al. (2015)** published the clearest demonstration of this in *The Journal of Physiology*. In a randomized crossover design, they had participants perform resistance training followed by either CWI or active recovery. At the cellular level, CWI attenuated satellite cell activity and downstream anabolic signalling. Over a 12-week training block, the CWI group showed significantly reduced gains in muscle mass and strength compared to the active recovery group. This was not a small, borderline finding — the adaptation differences were meaningful at the tissue and functional level.

**Yamane et al. (2006)** showed similar results over a 4-week resistance training intervention: CWI after sessions reduced strength adaptation compared to control conditions. The adaptation was there, but blunted.

**Peake et al. (2017)** published a systematic review in the *European Journal of Applied Physiology* synthesizing the CWI literature across recovery and adaptation outcomes. The conclusion is consistent with the mechanistic work: CWI attenuates the anabolic signalling and satellite cell response to resistance training, with downstream effects on long-term strength and hypertrophy gains.

The core tension is this: the same inflammatory response that CWI suppresses — producing less soreness and faster perceived recovery — is part of the signal that drives adaptation. When you blunt the inflammatory response, you reduce soreness. You also reduce the training signal.

**When CWI is defensible:** If you are in-season competing, with multiple competition events in close succession, and your priority is recovering well enough to perform at the next event rather than maximizing the training adaptation from this week's sessions, CWI is a reasonable tool. Recovery > adaptation is a legitimate in-season priority.

**When CWI is costing you gains:** If you are in an accumulation block — building strength and hypertrophy over a multi-week training block — regular post-training CWI is systematically attenuating the adaptations you are training to produce. You are paying the cost (hard sessions) without fully collecting the adaptation return.

The parallel to foam rolling is worth noting. Foam rolling reduces DOMS through gate control mechanisms; CWI reduces DOMS through vasoconstriction and reduced inflammatory signalling. Both feel like recovery. Only CWI carries a known cost to long-term adaptation.

**Bottom line: CWI post-resistance training blunts satellite cell activation and anabolic signalling, producing measurably attenuated hypertrophy and strength gains over a training block. Use it when you need to compete soon. Avoid it when you are in the middle of a strength-building phase.**

## Cold Environment Training: The Warm-Up Implication

Cold ambient temperature and cold water immersion have different physiological effects on training. Do not conflate them.

Training in a cold gym or cold outdoor environment primarily affects performance through reduced muscle temperature at the start of a session. As covered in the earlier section: cold muscle produces less force, has slower cross-bridge cycling rates, and carries higher injury risk at the connective tissue level. The Q10 effect means that enzyme kinetics slow meaningfully across the relevant temperature range.

The practical consequence is that warm-up becomes more critical in cold environments, not optional. The same athlete who might complete a 5-minute movement-based warm-up in a temperate gym needs a longer, more progressive warm-up when the environment starts cold — because the tissue needs more time to reach the temperature range where contractile performance is normal.

The specific risks of training with cold muscle:
- Reduced power output and peak force in early working sets
- Increased passive muscle stiffness, reducing the rate of force development
- Elevated risk of muscle and connective tissue strain under high-force loads before tissue temperature has normalized

Practical rule: in cold training environments, extend warm-up duration, include more progressive loading (do not jump to working weights after two warm-up sets when the gym is 10°C), and treat the warm-up as load management rather than formality.

Cold air does not produce the cardiovascular and immunological suppression that cold water immersion does. The peripheral cooling of cold air is real but far less acute than immersion. An athlete who trains in a cold garage and an athlete who gets in an ice bath post-session are experiencing physiologically distinct interventions — only the latter carries the adaptation attenuation risk.

**Bottom line: Cold training environments require a more thorough, progressive warm-up. Cold air and cold water immersion are different interventions with different physiological profiles — do not treat them as equivalent.**

## Heat for Recovery? The Sauna Data

Post-training sauna use is the inverse of CWI in several relevant ways, and the comparison is instructive.

The Laukkanen group (Laukkanen et al., multiple publications 2015–2018, *JAMA Internal Medicine* and *BMC Medicine*) has produced the most cited sauna epidemiology: regular sauna use in Finnish cohorts is associated with reduced cardiovascular mortality, dose-dependently across 2, 4, and 7 sessions per week. This is observational data with significant confounding (healthy user effect, socioeconomic factors, lifestyle clustering), so the direction of causality is not established. What it demonstrates is that regular sauna use is not harmful, and may be associated with positive cardiovascular markers.

For acute post-training sauna use, the physiological effects are: peripheral vasodilation, transiently elevated heart rate and cardiac output, a pulse in growth hormone secretion, and elevated core temperature. The GH response to sauna is real but modest in trained individuals, and the downstream anabolic contribution is likely small relative to training-driven GH release. Do not build a recovery strategy around the sauna GH response — the evidence does not support that as a primary mechanism.

What the sauna post-training does not do is suppress the inflammatory response that drives adaptation. Unlike CWI, which vasoconstricts and down-regulates inflammatory signalling, post-training sauna maintains or mildly enhances circulation without the specific attenuation of satellite cell activity and mTOR signalling documented with CWI. The sauna does not appear to blunt long-term hypertrophy or strength adaptation.

The evidence for sauna actively accelerating recovery is weaker than the evidence that it is benign. Vasodilation may aid metabolite clearance; subjective wellbeing after sauna is typically positive; heat shock protein induction has cellular protective functions. None of these are strong standalone reasons to program sauna specifically for recovery. The practical summary is: if you enjoy it and it helps you unwind post-session, it is not costing you anything. If you were considering replacing CWI with sauna for recovery, the adaptation profile is considerably better.

**Bottom line: Post-training sauna does not attenuate anabolic signalling — a meaningful distinction from CWI. The evidence for active recovery acceleration is modest. It is not harmful and may carry cardiovascular benefits with regular long-term use.**

## Practical Application

**Decision framework by training phase and goal:**

| Situation | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| In-season / competition within 48–72 hours | CWI is defensible — prioritize perceived recovery over adaptation |
| Accumulation block (building strength/hypertrophy) | Avoid post-training CWI; use active recovery, light movement, or sauna instead |
| Preparing for competition in hot conditions | 10-day exercise-heat acclimation protocol 2–3 weeks before event |
| Training in cold environment (ambient <12°C) | Extend warm-up to 10–15 min, progressive loading, prioritize tissue temperature before working sets |
| Post-training recovery, no competition imminent | Sauna, light walking, sleep quality — not CWI |
| Pre-training in warm environment (gym >28°C) | Manage hydration; the warmth itself aids contractile performance up to the threshold; core temp management during session if extreme |

**Warm-up by environment:**

- Temperate gym (18–24°C): 5–8 minutes dynamic warm-up, progressive warm-up sets
- Cold gym or outdoor (<15°C): 10–15 minutes; more progressive loading before working weight
- Hot conditions (>28°C): standard warm-up; prioritize hydration; monitor heat accumulation across session

**CWI if used strategically (in-season only):** 10–15°C, 10–15 minutes, within 30 minutes post-session, reserved for high-priority competition recovery windows.

**Heat acclimation (deliberate preparation):** 10–14 days, 30–60 minutes moderate exercise in 35–40°C ambient; or passive sauna over 4+ weeks. Schedule 2–4 weeks before target event. Marker of acclimation: resting HR reduction and reduced perceived exertion at standard workload.

**Bottom line: Temperature decisions should be phase-dependent. CWI is a competition recovery tool, not a training block staple. Cold training environments require more warm-up. Heat acclimation is a legitimate performance preparation protocol, not just an endurance concept.**

## Victor's Take

Cold water immersion is the temperature intervention most strength athletes use most carelessly — Roberts et al. (2015) is a clean, direct study and its result is consistent with the mechanistic picture: CWI blunts satellite cell activity, attenuates mTOR signalling, and produces measurably smaller strength and hypertrophy gains over a block. The rule is phase-dependent: if you have competition within 72 hours and recovery is the priority, CWI is defensible; if you are three weeks into a strength block, you are diluting the return on every session you have put in. Heat acclimation is underused by strength athletes who treat it as an endurance concept — plasma volume expansion and reduced cardiovascular strain have direct value in high-volume training blocks, and a 10-day protocol is a legitimate investment before a demanding accumulation phase. Temperature is not background noise; choose intentionally, phase-appropriately, and with the evidence rather than the locker room consensus.
