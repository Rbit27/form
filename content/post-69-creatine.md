# Creatine: The Mechanism, the Evidence, and the Cases Where It Does Not Matter

Creatine is the most studied ergogenic supplement in exercise science. The research base spans four decades. The position stands are about as settled as sports nutrition gets. And yet the supplement section at any gym retailer is full of "advanced creatine formulas," loading phase controversy, and products implying that monohydrate is somehow outdated.

This post covers what creatine actually does, what the evidence reliably shows for strength and hypertrophy, how the loading question breaks down, and — equally important — the training contexts where creatine probably does not matter enough to think about.

---

## The Mechanism: Why PCr Resynthesis Is the Relevant Biology

Understanding why creatine works requires understanding the phosphocreatine (PCr) system — the fastest ATP resynthesis pathway available to contracting muscle.

During maximal or near-maximal effort, your muscles need ATP faster than aerobic metabolism can produce it. Glycolysis can contribute, but it has a lag time and accumulates metabolic byproducts at high rates. The phosphocreatine system fills the gap in the first 5–10 seconds of maximal effort by doing something simple: phosphocreatine donates its phosphate group to ADP, regenerating ATP almost instantaneously.

**PCr + ADP → Creatine + ATP**

This reaction is catalysed by creatine kinase and occurs in the immediate vicinity of the myosin ATPase — the enzyme actually powering contraction. There is no lag, no byproduct accumulation, and the rate of ATP delivery is fast enough to sustain maximal force output briefly.

The constraint is capacity. Intramuscular PCr stores are finite. At true maximal intensity, PCr can sustain effort for approximately 8–10 seconds before depletion becomes a limiting factor. Beyond that point, force output declines as the muscle shifts toward slower resynthesis pathways.

PCr replenishment during recovery is relatively fast — approximately 95–99% restored within 3–5 minutes of rest at the intensities relevant to strength training. This is why rest interval length matters for power output in subsequent sets: PCr needs time to recharge.

**What creatine supplementation does is increase the total intramuscular PCr pool.** By providing more substrate for the phosphocreatine reaction, you expand the reservoir available during maximal effort. A larger reservoir means sustained high-intensity output for slightly longer before depletion limits force production — and potentially faster net resynthesis during rest intervals.

This is not a stimulant mechanism. Creatine does not increase arousal, alter pain perception, or change motivation. It changes the biochemistry of rapid energy resynthesis at the muscle level.

## The Evidence Base

### Strength

The strength evidence for creatine is robust and replicable. A 2003 meta-analysis by Rawson and Volek examined 16 studies and found that creatine supplementation produced approximately an 8% increase in strength compared to placebo across upper and lower body compound movements. The effect on repetitions-to-failure at a given load was larger — approximately 14% — suggesting creatine may extend work capacity within a set more than it shifts one-repetition maximum directly.

Lanhers et al. (2017) published a meta-analysis specifically examining creatine and upper body strength, including 22 studies. The finding: significant improvements in bench press 1RM and total repetition performance, with effect sizes in the moderate range. The benefit was consistent across untrained and trained populations, though training status modulates the absolute magnitude.

The International Society of Sports Nutrition position stand (Kreider et al., 2017) synthesised the accumulated literature and concluded: creatine monohydrate is the most effective ergogenic nutritional supplement currently available for increasing high-intensity exercise capacity and lean body mass during training. This is as close to a consensus statement as sports nutrition produces.

The strength effect is not subtle and it is not speculative. It is one of the most replicated findings in the field.

### Hypertrophy

The hypertrophy picture is more nuanced. Creatine does not directly stimulate muscle protein synthesis in the way that training-induced mechanical tension and anabolic signalling cascades do. The hypertrophy benefit is largely indirect — mediated through training capacity.

If creatine allows you to complete more total volume at a given intensity (more reps, slightly heavier loads, better recovery between sets), the downstream hypertrophic stimulus is larger. More volume under tension, assuming adequate recovery and protein availability, produces more adaptation.

There is some evidence for direct cellular mechanisms — creatine may increase cell volumisation (intramuscular water content), which is associated with anabolic signalling in some research, and it may support satellite cell activity and glycogen synthesis. But the magnitude of these effects independent of training volume is modest compared to the volume-mediated pathway.

Practical implication: creatine supports hypertrophy primarily by extending high-quality training capacity. It is most effective when you are actually using that additional capacity — training hard enough to be limited by phosphocreatine availability.

### Non-Responders

Approximately 25–30% of people experience minimal measurable benefit from creatine supplementation. The most likely explanation is baseline intramuscular creatine saturation. Individuals who consume significant quantities of red meat (which contains creatine) may already have higher resting PCr stores, leaving less room for supplementation to produce a measurable shift. Vegetarians and vegans, who have little to no dietary creatine intake, tend to show larger responses — their baseline stores are typically lower, meaning the supplementation produces a more substantial increase relative to starting point.

This is not a failure of the supplement. It is baseline physiology. If you consume a kilogram of beef daily and see minimal effect from creatine supplementation, you are not a defect in the data.

## Loading vs. Maintenance: What the Protocol Actually Looks Like

The "loading phase" debate generates more gym controversy than it warrants. Here is what the evidence shows.

**Loading protocol:** 20 grams per day divided into four 5-gram doses for 5–7 days, followed by a maintenance dose of 3–5 grams per day. This reaches intramuscular saturation within approximately one week.

**Maintenance-only protocol:** 3–5 grams per day from the outset. This reaches the same intramuscular saturation as loading — it simply takes longer, approximately 28 days instead of one week.

There is no evidence that loading produces superior long-term outcomes compared to lower daily dosing. If you reach the same saturation level in 28 days, the training effects are equivalent from that point forward. The loading phase accelerates the time to saturation. That is its only function.

If you have a competitive event in 10 days and want creatine benefits by then, loading makes sense. If you are planning a 16-week training block with no acute deadline, loading provides no sustained advantage.

**Gastrointestinal tolerance** is the practical argument against loading for some people. Twenty grams per day divided into four doses is generally tolerable. Consuming it in fewer, larger doses (10 grams at once) increases the risk of gastrointestinal discomfort. The dose-splitting protocol exists for this reason, not because of any biochemical necessity of the split itself.

## Timing: Does It Matter When You Take It?

Two studies addressed creatine timing directly and produced results that have been over-interpreted ever since.

Antonio and Ciccone (2013) compared post-workout versus pre-workout creatine in 19 recreational bodybuilders over four weeks and found a small advantage for post-workout supplementation on lean mass gains. Candow et al. (2014) found a similar non-significant trend toward benefit from consumption closer to training.

**The honest read of this data:** effect sizes were small, sample sizes were modest, and neither study represents a definitive finding. Nutrient timing for creatine matters orders of magnitude less than consistent daily intake. Missing your post-workout window but maintaining your 3–5 grams daily is vastly preferable to optimising timing while having inconsistent daily intake.

Take it when it fits your routine. Consistency over weeks and months is what produces intramuscular saturation and maintains it.

## Creatine Forms: Monohydrate Is Not Outdated

The supplement industry generates revenue by selling the idea that monohydrate is inferior to newer proprietary forms. The evidence does not support this.

**Creatine monohydrate** is the form used in essentially all the research cited above. Its absorption, bioavailability, and efficacy are well-characterised. It is inexpensive and stable.

**Creatine HCl (hydrochloride)** is marketed as more soluble and requiring a lower dose. Solubility is irrelevant to absorption at the gut level in a well-hydrated individual, and no human trial has demonstrated superior performance outcomes compared to monohydrate at equivalent doses.

**Buffered creatine (Kre-Alkalyn)** claims to resist conversion to creatinine in the stomach. The conversion rate of monohydrate to creatinine in the stomach is already low and functionally insignificant for bioavailability. The buffered form has not outperformed monohydrate in head-to-head comparison.

**Creatine ethyl ester** showed worse bioavailability than monohydrate in direct comparison (Spillane et al., 2009).

The premium you pay for "advanced" creatine formulas funds marketing infrastructure, not evidence-based performance superiority. Buy monohydrate. The research was done on monohydrate. There is no mechanism by which an alternative form improves on a well-absorbed, well-characterised compound that already reaches near-complete muscle saturation.

## Water Retention: What Actually Happens

Creatine supplementation causes an initial increase in total body water, typically in the range of 0.5–1.5 kilograms, particularly during and after a loading phase. This is real and worth understanding accurately.

The water retained is **intramuscular** — drawn into the muscle cell alongside creatine as part of osmotic equilibrium. This is distinct from subcutaneous water retention (which would produce a "soft" appearance under the skin). Intramuscular water does not substantially alter visual appearance.

For athletes in weight-class sports, this initial water increase matters practically: competing within one week of starting creatine could place you above your target weight class. The solution is either to begin supplementation after a weigh-in, time the loading phase well in advance, or simply use maintenance dosing from the outset to allow a more gradual shift.

For general training with no weight class constraint, intramuscular water retention is a non-issue. It represents cellular hydration associated with increased PCr stores, not a confound to be managed.

## Safety: The Actual Record

Creatine has been used in research and by athletes for over 30 years. The safety profile in healthy individuals is well-established.

**Kidney function** is the most common concern raised about creatine, and it is not supported by the evidence in healthy individuals. Bizzarini and De Angelis (2004) reviewed the renal safety literature and found no credible evidence of kidney damage in people without pre-existing renal conditions. Subsequent long-term studies have not overturned this conclusion.

The confusion arises partly from creatinine — the metabolic byproduct of creatine metabolism — being a marker used to assess kidney function. Creatine supplementation increases urinary creatinine excretion, which can produce a mildly elevated creatinine reading in blood tests. This is not renal damage; it is normal excretion of a higher creatine turnover. Clinicians and athletes should be aware of this confound when interpreting bloodwork.

**Individuals with pre-existing kidney conditions** should consult a physician before supplementing. The general population in good health has no credible renal risk from creatine at evidence-based doses.

Gastrointestinal discomfort (bloating, cramping) occurs in some individuals at loading doses and is dose-dependent. Splitting doses and ensuring adequate hydration typically resolves this.

## When Creatine Does Not Matter

Creatine is often discussed as though it belongs in every training programme. There are contexts where it is unlikely to produce meaningful benefit worth optimising.

**Training phases dominated by technique work at submaximal intensity.** If you are spending a training block rebuilding squat mechanics at 50–65% of your working weight, you are not approaching phosphocreatine system limits. The mechanism requires near-maximal effort to be a limiting factor. At submaximal intensity with volume as the stressor, PCr depletion is not the bottleneck.

**Aerobic-dominant training.** Creatine's mechanism is specific to high-intensity, short-duration efforts. The phosphocreatine system contributes minimally to aerobic endurance performance measured over longer time domains. The evidence for creatine benefit in endurance athletes is weak compared to strength athletes, though there is some evidence for interval-heavy training within endurance programmes.

**Already near-saturated from dietary intake.** Individuals consuming substantial daily quantities of red meat have meaningfully higher baseline PCr stores. The incremental benefit of supplementation is proportionally smaller. This is not a definitive reason to avoid creatine — you may still benefit — but it is a reason not to expect the full response documented in vegetarians or typical recreationally active individuals.

**The first four weeks of any programme, for beginners.** Novice trainees adapt rapidly to any training stimulus through neural mechanisms — improved motor unit recruitment, coordination, reduced inhibition — that have nothing to do with PCr availability. The relative contribution of creatine to strength gains in the first weeks of training is small compared to the neural adaptation signal. This will change as the lifter progresses, but creatine is not where a beginner's attention is best directed first.

**When consistency of training and sleep are not established.** Creatine does not compensate for training gaps, inadequate recovery, or insufficient protein intake. It is a marginal gain on top of a functional training programme. If the programme is not yet functional, the marginal gain is marginal relative to what establishing the programme would produce.

## What the Evidence Actually Supports

Creatine monohydrate at 3–5 grams per day, taken consistently, increases intramuscular phosphocreatine stores and produces measurable improvements in high-intensity exercise capacity — including strength, power, and work volume within training sessions. The mechanism is well-understood. The safety record in healthy individuals is extensive. The cost is low. The form that all the research was conducted on is still the form that works.

Loading is optional. Timing is not a significant variable. Proprietary alternatives have not demonstrated superiority. Non-response exists and is explained by baseline physiology.

Creatine is not magic. It is a modest, reliable, well-characterised tool that works through a specific mechanism. Its usefulness depends on whether that mechanism is actually a limiting factor in your training.

If you are training at intensities where phosphocreatine depletion limits your output — and most strength athletes do, on most heavy sets — then creatine is likely worth taking. If you are not in that category, or if more foundational variables remain unaddressed, start there first.

The tool does what it does. Know what that is before deciding whether it belongs in your programme.
