## Everything you read about training is right. None of it is about you.

There's a specific frustration that comes from being a serious training enthusiast who reads the literature.

You find a well-designed study. Decent sample size, controlled variables, pre-registered. It says: subjects who trained with protocol X gained Y% more strength than the control group. The study looks fine. You try the protocol. Nothing happens — or at least, not what the study promised.

This has happened to me. It's happened to almost every serious trainee I've talked to. And most people respond in one of two bad ways.

The first: conclude that the research is useless, and return to intuition and inherited gym lore. The second: conclude that you're doing something wrong, and try harder to match the protocol exactly.

Neither of these responses is correct.

---

**The issue isn't the research**

The study was probably fine. The result was real — for that population.

The problem is what "real for that population" actually means in practice.

Sports science research is conducted predominantly on untrained or recreationally active subjects. This happens for practical reasons: they're easier to recruit, they show faster and cleaner results, and the effects are larger, making them easier to detect with modest sample sizes.

If you've trained consistently for two or three years, you are not that subject. Your neural adaptation profile is different. Your connective tissue state is different. Your response window to volume and intensity changes is different. Applying a finding calculated across people who had never done systematic resistance training to someone with three years of consistent training history is a category error — not a mistake in the research, but a mistake in how it's being applied.

The research isn't wrong. It just isn't about you.

---

**The individual response problem**

Here's what the literature makes clear but practitioners rarely discuss in plain terms.

Individual response to training varies enormously — even within well-designed studies with homogeneous populations doing identical programs.

One frequently cited hypertrophy study tracked muscle cross-sectional area changes in subjects following the same protocol. The range of individual outcomes was roughly 5% to 59%. The study average sat somewhere in the middle. That average was, for a substantial portion of subjects, nothing like their actual result.

If you're a non-responder to high-frequency training, a meta-analysis showing that frequency matters will not help you. If you're a strong responder to volume, conservative starting recommendations will leave you chronically undertrained.

The mean isn't you. For any given training variable, it is never you — not because you're unusual, but because individual variation is built into biology and a mean is a statistical construct, not a person.

---

**What evidence-based frameworks get wrong**

Most "evidence-based training" frameworks take population-level data and convert it into individual prescriptions without flagging that this is what they're doing.

"Research suggests you should perform 10–20 sets per muscle per week."

The sentence is not technically wrong. But the confidence interval on that optimal range is large, the range itself is population-derived, and the actual optimal for a specific individual at a specific training age could sit well outside it in either direction. The research gives you a reasonable starting point — a prior, in statistical terms. It does not give you your individual optimal.

The correct posture toward training research is: use it to form priors, not prescriptions. A prior is a starting assumption you're willing to update when evidence contradicts it. A prescription is a rule you follow regardless of how your training actually responds.

If you treat research as priors, your training log becomes the update mechanism. You start where the evidence suggests. You observe what happens across weeks, not workouts. You adjust when the signal is clear. Over time, your log accumulates an n-of-1 dataset that is far more specific to you than any population-level study could be.

---

**The part that requires patience**

This approach requires a specific kind of discipline that is different from the discipline of following a program.

Most training logs aren't functional n-of-1 experiments. They're incomplete, inconsistent, or tracking variables that don't support the conclusions you'd want to draw. Six months of "bench press 3×8, felt tired" doesn't tell you whether you'd respond better to higher frequency or higher volume.

A training log that actually functions as evidence requires:

- Load and volume recorded precisely enough to detect trends across weeks
- Brief subjective markers: sleep quality, readiness, external stress — not a diary, just enough context
- Performance tracked over meaningful time windows: four to eight weeks for strength trends, longer for structural adaptation
- Protocol changes noted as events, so you can correlate changes in stimulus with changes in outcome

This is what FORM is being built for. Not to tell you what the research says and convert it to a plan on your behalf. But to help you run your own n-of-1 with enough structure, consistency, and contextual data to draw real conclusions about how your training actually works.

Victor doesn't know your individual response profile before you train. Neither do we. But the log builds it — and that's what makes coaching possible over time.

---

**One shift that makes research more useful immediately**

If you want to start applying research more accurately before anything else, here's the simplest change:

When you read a study finding you're tempted to apply, ask two questions.

First: who were the subjects? Untrained? Recreationally active? Competitive athletes? What age range, training history, and baseline fitness level? The further the subjects are from you, the weaker the direct application.

Second: how large was the individual variation? Was the standard deviation large relative to the mean? Did some subjects show responses in the opposite direction from the group average?

These two questions will filter out a large proportion of the noise in training advice. What remains is still just priors — but better ones. More relevant, more honest about their uncertainty.
